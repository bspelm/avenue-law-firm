Understanding Quit Claim Deed Basics in NY

When it comes to transferring ownership of real estate from one party to another in New York, a quit claim deed is one of the many different types of deeds that can be used. This official document transfers any legal rights in property that the grantor (the person transferring the stake) may have without a guarantee or warranty of title status. A quit claim deed is often used when the parties involved in the transfer are familiar with each other and there’s no question about whether or not the grantor has clear title to the property.


A quit claim deed is an official, written document that transfers any ownership rights that the grantor may have in a property to the grantee (the person receiving the property). The document needs to include information about the parties involved in the transaction, including their names and addresses. It should also provide a legal description of the property and the county where it’s located, along with the date of the transfer. Finally, if there was any cost associated with the transfer, this should be included in the document as well.

The document must be signed by the grantor and the grantee. In addition, the signatures must be notarized. Once completed, the quit claim deed should be recorded with your local county recorder’s office. There may be a recording fee charged, which is typically based on the amount of text in the deed. Recording it ensures that the transfer is officially noted in public records, which is crucial for future title searches.

In most cases, a quit claim deed does not affect any preexisting mortgages on the property. However, it’s important to check with your lender before executing this type of transfer. The lender may require the grantor to sign a special form of release or quit claim in order to remove the lien from the property record. Such a precaution with a quit claim deed ensures that all parties understand the financial responsibilities tied to the property.

Another reason why a quit claim deed is popular is that it allows people to avoid probate, which is the court-supervised process for distributing assets after someone dies. Using it to transfer property to a beneficiary or living trust before the grantor’s death can allow them to bypass probate and avoid costly and time-consuming litigation. This aspect of a quit claim deed is particularly appealing for those planning their estate.

Although a quit claim deed is an efficient and quick way to convey property, it doesn’t come with any guarantees about the property’s title. For this reason, it’s often only used when the transferring parties are familiar with each other, such as when parents or spouses give their children property that they previously owned jointly in the event of divorce or when a person transfers their property to an LLC. It’s also not ideal for transferring ownership to a bank or financial institution, since the document doesn’t include a promise of clear title. Instead, a warranty deed would be the preferred option for these situations. If you’re unsure which deed is right for your situation, consult an experienced attorney. They can explain the differences between the various types of deeds and help you choose the appropriate one.

Filing Quit Claim Deeds in NY

A quitclaim deed is a legal document used to transfer real property ownership from one party to another. This article will provide a step-by-step guide on how to file a quitclaim deed in New York.

The first step in filing a quitclaim deed is to obtain the proper documentation. This can be done by visiting your local county clerk’s office or by searching online for the correct forms. Once the paperwork has been obtained, it must be filled out completely and correctly. It is also important that it is signed and notarized in order to ensure that the signatures are authentic. Filing a quit claim deed without the correct documentation can lead to legal issues down the line.

Once the quit claim deed has been completed, it must be filed with the county clerk’s office in order to become a record of public record. In addition, the county clerk may have additional submission requirements that need to be met in order for the deed to be deemed valid. These requirements could include submitting additional documentation or paying certain fees. Ensuring that all the necessary steps are followed during the filing process is crucial to avoid any complications.

The next step in filing a quit claim deed in New York is to pay any necessary taxes. This includes recording fees, property transfer taxes, and any mortgage payoff balances if applicable. Failure to pay these taxes can result in significant penalties and fines. It's important to be aware of the financial obligations associated with the transfer of property through a quitclaim deed.

It is also a good idea to consider purchasing title insurance. This will protect the grantee in case there are any issues with the deed that were not discovered prior to the recording process. This policy will cover any expenses incurred in order to resolve any potential problems with the deed. Title insurance provides peace of mind to both parties involved in the transfer.

In New York, the recording process for a quitclaim deed is very similar to other real estate titles. Typically, the deed will need to contain the following information: the grantor’s full name and address; the consideration or exchange of real property; the legal description of the property; and the vesting (how the property is owned). Ensuring that these details are accurately included in the deed is essential for a valid transfer.
